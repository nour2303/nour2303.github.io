How I think about data

Short lessons, earned the honest way — from 276K+ rows of real, messy, sometimes fabricated data. Each one changed how I work.

01

Validate before you analyse — half a dataset can be fake

In my bike sales audit, the numbers looked fine at first glance. Then one simple question broke the whole thing open: where are the February 29th sales? Real multi-year sales data always has leap-day transactions — and a huge block of my 113,000 rows had none. Row-matching confirmed it: roughly half the dataset was fabricated, copied from real rows with the values changed.

And here's the part I'm most proud of: I didn't delete a single fake row. Every one stayed in the Excel workbook — flagged, and excluded by formulas only. Anyone can open the file today, see exactly which rows were flagged and why, and challenge my call. A finding you can audit is worth far more than a finding you have to take on faith. Since that project, no dataset gets a dashboard from me before it earns my trust.

From the Bike Sales Profitability Audit

02

Don't just describe the problem — find the lever

"Revenue is down 13%" is a fact. It's not a finding. In my Amazon analysis, revenue was falling month after month, and the easy move was to report twenty observations and let the stakeholder sort it out. Digging deeper showed that one category — 'Set', half the business — was behind roughly 81% of the entire drop. That one number changes the meeting: instead of "sales are declining," the conversation becomes "fix this one category and you recover most of the loss." Stakeholders don't need more charts. They need to know which lever to pull first.

From the Amazon Sales Analysis

03

Discounting looks like growth — until you check the margin

Revenue dashboards love discounts: every promotion makes the line go up, so discounting feels like a growth lever. The margin tells a different story. On verified bike sales data, profitability fell from 51% at full price to 31% at deep discounts — the business was buying volume and quietly paying for it in profit. And the bestseller wasn't the best earner: helmets made ~59% margin while the bikes themselves did the heavy, low-margin lifting. If you only track revenue, discounting looks like success. Track margin, and it's often a leak.

From the Bike Sales Profitability Audit

04

AI reads 35,000 reviews in a minute — but it still needs an analyst

Reading 35,000 written guest reviews would take a human weeks — and spotting the patterns across them, even longer. With AI-assisted text mining, it took less than a minute: thousands of free-text complaints turned into a ranked chart of exactly why guests were unhappy — cleanliness, bathrooms, smell, bugs. That's work that simply wasn't practical before, done at the speed of a coffee break.

But AI didn't replace the analyst's eye. While cleaning, I noticed the rating column didn't behave like a 1–5 scale — some scores reached 10. I investigated and found the source had mixed a 1–5 and a 1–10 rating scale, traced the off-scale ratings to a specific handful of hotels, and isolated them — leaving a clean 1–5 dataset of ~33,800 rated reviews. Without catching this, every average rating would have been wrong. That's the combination I work with: AI for scale, human judgement for trust.

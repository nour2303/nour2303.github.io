How I think about data

Short lessons, earned the honest way — from 276K+ rows of real, messy, sometimes fabricated data. Each one changed how I work.

01

Validate before you analyse — half a dataset can be fake

In my bike sales audit, ~50% of 113,000 rows turned out to be fabricated. A simple calendar fact exposed it: real multi-year sales data has transactions on 29 February, and the fake rows didn't. If I had skipped the audit, every chart would have been polished, confident — and wrong. Now no analysis of mine starts before the data has earned my trust.

From the Bike Sales Profitability Audit

02

Detractor rate catches failing hotels faster than averages

Averages are slow. A hotel can absorb hundreds of furious reviews and barely move its mean score, because the happy majority dilutes the signal. The share of clearly unhappy guests — the detractor rate — reacts immediately. Across 626 hotels, it flagged properties in trouble while their averages still looked respectable; at the worst hotel, 90% of all reviews were 1–2 stars. Choose metrics for how fast they detect change, not how familiar they look.

From the Hotel Reputation Analysis

03

Discounting is the silent profit leak

Discounts feel like a growth lever because revenue keeps climbing. The margin tells another story: in my bike sales analysis, profitability eroded from 51% at full price to 31% at deep discounts — and the bestselling product wasn't the most profitable one (helmets, at ~59% margin, out-earned the bikes). Revenue dashboards hide this; margin dashboards expose it.

From the Bike Sales Profitability Audit

04

The numbers say "what" — only the text says "why"

A 2-star rating tells you a guest is unhappy, not what to fix. Mining ~35,000 free-text reviews with AI assistance turned vague unhappiness into a ranked, fixable list — with the hygiene-and-condition cluster (cleanliness, bathrooms, smell, bugs) far outweighing everything else. Unstructured text is where the actionable insight lives, and modern AI tooling finally makes it cheap to harvest at scale.

How I think about data

Short lessons I earned the honest way, from 276K+ rows of real and messy data that was sometimes even fabricated. Each one changed how I work.

01

Validate before you analyse — half a dataset can be fake

In my bike sales audit the numbers looked fine at first glance. Then one simple question broke the whole thing open: where are the February 29th sales? Real multi-year sales data always has leap-day transactions, and a huge block of my 113,000 rows had none. Row-matching confirmed it, and roughly half the dataset turned out to be fabricated, copied from real rows with the values changed.

One thing I was careful about was flagging the fake rows instead of deleting them. Removing data isn't a call I make on my own, so you keep it until someone confirms it should go. The rows stayed in the workbook and were left out of the analysis by formula, but they're still there for anyone to check. Since that project no dataset gets a dashboard from me before it earns my trust.

From the Bike Sales Profitability Audit

02

Don't just describe the problem — find the lever

"Revenue is down 13%" is a fact and not a finding. In my Amazon analysis revenue was falling month after month, and the easy move would have been to hand over twenty observations and let the stakeholder sort it out. Digging deeper showed that a single category called 'Set', which was half the whole business, sat behind roughly 81% of the entire drop. That one number changes the whole meeting. Instead of "sales are declining" the conversation becomes "fix this one category and you recover most of the loss". Stakeholders don't need more charts. They need to know which lever to pull first.

From the Amazon Sales Analysis

03

Discounting looks like growth — until you check the margin

Revenue dashboards love discounts. Every promotion makes the line go up, so discounting feels like a growth lever. The margin tells a very different story. On the verified bike sales data profitability fell from 51% at full price down to 31% at deep discounts, which meant the business was buying volume and quietly paying for it in profit. The bestseller wasn't even the best earner, since helmets made about 59% margin while the bikes did the heavy low-margin lifting. If you only track revenue discounting looks like success, but track margin and it's often a leak.

From the Bike Sales Profitability Audit

04

AI reads thousands of reviews in a minute — but it still needs an analyst

Reading thousands of written guest reviews one by one would take a person weeks, and finding the common threads across them would take even longer. Using AI I scanned them all in a fraction of that time and turned what I found into a chart. The big finding was that most complaints came down to cleanliness and room condition.

But the part that mattered most came before any of that. The ratings were meant to be 1 to 5 stars, yet I noticed some went up to 10. Tracing it down, the problem came from just 5 hotels out of 626 that had been recorded on a 1-to-10 scale by mistake. Three of them were cleanly on the 1-to-10 scale so I converted those down to 1-to-5, while the other two were a confusing mix of both so I left them out rather than guess. Without that catch, every average rating would have been wrong. AI is fast, but it takes a person to notice when the numbers themselves can't be trusted.

From the Hotel Reputation Analysis

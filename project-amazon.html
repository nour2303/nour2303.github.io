Amazon Sales Analysis
₹8.3M was leaking. The decline was accelerating.

An end-to-end analytics build on 131,146 raw rows of Amazon India sales data from April to June 2022. I cleaned and quality-assured it in MySQL, modelled it into a star schema with 17 DAX measures and delivered a two-page interactive Power BI dashboard alongside a stakeholder report with prioritised actions.

The problem

Three months of sales brought in ₹69.6M of realized revenue from roughly 107,000 completed orders. It looked healthy at first glance, but revenue fell every single month (₹25.7M → ₹23.5M → ₹20.4M) and the decline kept speeding up (−8.6%, then −12.9%). On top of that another ₹8.3M of potential revenue leaked away through cancellations and returns. So the real questions were what's driving the fall and where exactly the money is going.

Cleaning before analysing

The raw export was far from analysis-ready. Before any conclusions, 131,146 messy rows were reduced to a reliable 127,200-row table:

  • 2,210 exact duplicates and 1,754 fully-empty records removed (~3% of the file), plus a junk column dropped.
  • 6,959 blank courier statuses resolved: 5,205 rows with valid sale amounts were inferred as "Shipped", an imputation that doesn't touch revenue figures.
  • Types and headers fixed: amount and date columns arrived as text; 24 messy headers standardised to snake_case. Result: zero nulls, zero duplicates, single consistent currency.

The approach

  • SQL (MySQL) for analysis: profiling, CTEs, window functions and conditional aggregation to break revenue down by category, geography, channel and time.
  • A leakage framework: separating realized revenue from cancelled (₹6.9M) and returned/lost (₹1.4M) orders, so the topline and the leak could be tracked independently.
  • A star-schema Power BI model: date dimension and 17 DAX measures (revenue, leakage, rates, AOV, time-intelligence), each one double-checked against the SQL source so every number matched exactly.
  • A two-page interactive dashboard: KPIs, trend, category, geography and leakage views with slicers, built for stakeholders to self-serve.

What it found

  • The 'Set' category was collapsing: down 31% over the quarter. As half the business (₹34.7M), it drove ~81% of the entire revenue drop. Rescuing it is the single biggest lever (~₹4M).
  • Western Dress was the growth engine: up 27% while everything else shrank, at a healthy ₹791 order value.
  • Cancellations hit 13% of orders, with two regional hotspots well above the norm: Kerala (16.5%) and Andhra Pradesh (15.2%).
  • Concentration risk: 91% of revenue sat in just three categories, and B2B orders, though small, spent more and cancelled far less (7.9% vs 13%).
The dashboard didn't just show that revenue was falling. It showed that one category's collapse explained 81% of the fall and even named the two states where cancellations were bleeding the most.

Why it matters

Every number a stakeholder sees traces back through a validated DAX measure to a SQL query on cleaned data. The report turns each finding into an action, whether that's rescuing 'Set', fuelling Western Dress or attacking the two cancellation hotspots, with the money value of each lever attached. Scope note: cost data wasn't available, so this measures revenue and order quality rather than net profit, and the report says so plainly.

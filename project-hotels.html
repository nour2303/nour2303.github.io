Hotel Reputation Analysis
33,783 reviews. One root cause. 14 hotels to fix first.

Roughly 35,900 raw guest reviews across 626 hotels, taken from a broken database import to a three-page Power BI dashboard and a stakeholder report — proving that weak reputation concentrates in budget brands and is driven overwhelmingly by cleanliness and room condition.

SQL (MySQL) Power BI AI text mining Self-directed · 2026

The problem

A portfolio of 626 hotels, six years of guest reviews (2011–2016), and an overall rating of 3.76 / 5 that had been flat for years. No crisis on the surface — but averages hide structure. Which hotels are actually failing, why, and what should be fixed first?

Earning the data's trust first

  • Caught a silent import failure — the standard MySQL import loaded only 764 of ~35,900 rows without erroring. Diagnosed the truncation, scripted a reliable bulk load, verified the count, and fixed character encoding so international hotel names rendered correctly.
  • Caught a mixed rating scale — the rating column didn't behave like 1–5; some scores reached 10. A few hotels had been recorded on a 1–10 scale by mistake. Three were cleanly on 1–10, so I converted those down to 1–5 and kept them; the other two were a confusing mix of both scales, so I left them out rather than guess. With ~300 junk zero-rated records and 304 duplicates also removed, that left a clean 1–5 dataset of 33,783 reviews. Without this catch, every average would have been wrong.
  • Set a fairness threshold — only hotels with 10+ reviews get ranked, so one angry guest can't sink a hotel's standing.

The approach

  • Look past the average — tracked the detractor rate (share of 1–2 star reviews) alongside the mean. At one hotel, 90% of all reviews were 1–2 stars while portfolio averages looked calm.
  • AI-assisted text mining at scale — read ~35,000 rows of free-text review content to detect and count recurring complaint themes: cleanliness, bathrooms, odour, noise, pests — turning unstructured text into a ranked chart of why guests were unhappy.
  • Five reputation tiers — every ranked hotel classified Superb → Terrible on a Booking.com-style scale, plus year-over-year movement to spot risers and decliners.
  • Three-page Power BI dashboard — executive overview, reputation trends, and a brand / root-cause deep dive, backed by DAX measures and calculated tables.

What it found

  • Reputation tracks the price tier almost perfectly — upscale brands average ~4★+; budget chains cluster at the bottom. The problem is structural, which is why a brand-level fix beats chasing individual hotels.
  • Hygiene and room condition drive the anger — in 1–2 star reviews, cleanliness, bathrooms, smell and bugs together far outweigh everything else. Guests forgive "basic"; they don't forgive "dirty".
  • 14 priority hotels were named — including one averaging 1.46★ over 533 reviews, with nearly 9 in 10 guests leaving a detractor review.
  • The problem is fixable — one budget-brand hotel gained almost a full star in a single year, proving the tier isn't doomed.
A targeted problem with a targeted fix, not a portfolio-wide crisis: one brand-level cleanliness programme reaches hundreds of properties at once — the single highest-leverage move available.

Why it matters

This project combines skills that rarely sit together: forensic data validation (the import and rating-scale catches), a metric choice that detects failure earlier (detractor rate over averages), and modern AI-assisted analysis of unstructured text — all delivered as prioritised actions a hotel operations team could start on Monday.
